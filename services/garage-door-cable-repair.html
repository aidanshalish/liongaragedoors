Cables are the unsung heroes of a garage door system. They shoulder the load quietly until rust, fraying, or misalignment stops the show. I’ve spent more than forty years nursing cables back into service across York Region. At sixty-five, my back might grumble if I forget to stretch, but my focus staying sharp is non-negotiable. When a cable lets go, I’m there with fresh lines, sturdy fasteners, and a calm voice that settles nerves.

The moment I arrive, I secure the door. A hanging door can be dangerous, so I clamp it, support it with blocks, or lower it safely to the ground. I inspect drums, bearings, and brackets, looking for wear patterns. If one cable has snapped, the other might be moments away. I’ve learnt to spot kinks, flattened strands, and corrosion hiding near the bottom fixtures where salt collects.

I carry premium aircraft-grade cables in different thicknesses and lengths. We match the cable to your door’s weight, spring system, and drum size. I measure twice before cutting and use proper ferrules, not makeshift knots. Drums get cleaned with a wire brush, and set screws are tightened with torque that keeps them put without chewing the shaft. A cable that’s wound neatly will run smooth for years.

Many calls involve bird-nesting—cables jumping the drum and wrapping around the shaft. That usually happens when a door hits an obstruction or the spring tension is uneven. I unwind the mess carefully, reset the balance, and check that the tracks are plumb. If rollers bind or hinges bend, I address those culprits so the new cables don’t suffer the same fate.

Bottom brackets and safety tabs are critical. I replace any bracket that shows cracks or warping. On older doors without tamper-resistant hardware, I install upgraded brackets and safety cables. I’d rather spend a few extra minutes now than risk a cable whipping loose later. When the Ontario cold snaps arrive, secure hardware prevents brittle metal from splitting.

Once the new cables are wound and tensioned, we test the door manually. It should lift smoothly and stay put halfway without drifting. I adjust spring tension as needed, listening for subtle cues—a creak here, a tug there. Then we reconnect the opener, run the door through a full cycle, and let it rest gently on the floor. If anything rattles, I keep tuning until silence returns.

I leave behind guidance on caring for your cables: keep the tracks clean, avoid spraying salt-heavy slush, and call me if the door ever jerks or tilts. You’ll receive a handwritten note with the cable length, thickness, and installation date. Sometimes my writing drops an apostrophe—“dont” slips out when I’m writing fast—but the information stays spot-on. I phone a week later to make sure the door still runs true.

Cable repair might seem like a small detail, yet it’s the difference between a dependable door and a hazard. Trust a seasoned technician who respects the tension, follows safety steps faithfully, and treats every component like it belongs to his own home. Together we’ll keep your door lifting evenly and your household moving smoothly.

Call me anytime, day or night. I’ll show up with steady hands, new cables, and the same neighbourly care I’ve offered since the seventies.
