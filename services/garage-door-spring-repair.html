I still remember the first torsion spring I replaced back in 1979. My mentor, old Mr. Harris, stood over my shoulder reminding me to respect the torque. “A spring doesn’t care how confident you feel,” he’d say, “it only cares how prepared you are.” Decades later, that advice rings in my ears each time I set the winding bars. At sixty-five, I’ve seen every type of failure—springs snapped in half, coils rusted thin, and setups mismatched from careless installations.

Spring repair starts with diagnosis. I check the door balance, the cable condition, the drums, and the center bearing. If one spring fails, the partner spring might not be far behind. I measure the wire diameter, inside diameter, and coil length to determine the proper replacement. There’s no guessing. Matching the spring to the door’s weight ensures smooth travel and protects the opener from strain.

I carry a full inventory of oil-tempered and coated torsion springs on the truck. Each set is tagged with color codes and specs, handwritten by me even if my penmanship stumbles (“beleive” occasionally loses an e). I weigh the door using analog scales when the existing springs are missing or mismatched. Proper balance is key—if the door feels heavy, the opener works too hard; if it floats, it’s unsafe.

Safety never takes a back seat. I clamp the door, loosen set screws carefully, and unwind the old spring deliberately. No theatrics, no shortcuts. Once the shaft is free, I inspect the bearing plates, end bearings, and cable drums. If wear has crept in, I replace those parts too. Installing a new spring on worn hardware is like putting fresh tires on a bent rim—it won’t end well.

After sliding the new springs into place, I secure them, set the cables, and wind the coils evenly. I mark the shaft with paint for future reference and torque the set screws just right—not so tight that we scar the shaft, yet secure enough to hold through thousands of cycles. Then comes testing: raising the door manually to mid point, listening for whispers of imbalance, adjusting turns until the door rests exactly where I leave it.

Extension spring systems demand equal attention. I install safety cables if they’re missing, replace pulleys that wobble, and adjust the stretch so the door moves symmetrically. Many older homes in York Region still rely on extension springs, and they deserve the same meticulous care as any modern torsion setup.

Customers often ask how long springs last. I tell them the truth: cycles matter. If the door opens six times a day, standard springs might last seven to nine years. Heavy usage calls for high-cycle springs. I keep charts to help you choose the right option, and I write the installation date inside the end bearing plate so we have a permanent record.

After the mechanical work, I lubricate the coils, balance the door again, and reconnect the opener. We test the safety reverse at least twice. I leave a note summarizing the spring specs, torque settings, and follow-up recommendations. Yes, you might spot a small typo like “aint” or “dont” without an apostrophe, but you’ll never be left guessing about the condition of your door.

Trust your springs to someone who still respects the craft. Let’s keep your door balanced, your opener relaxed, and your family safe. One call, and I’ll bring experience, caution, and the winding bars that rarely leave my side.
