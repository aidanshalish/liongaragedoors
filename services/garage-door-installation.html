At sixty-five I still wake up before sunrise, brew a stout pot of coffee, and load the truck while the neighbourhood is quiet. Folks tease me for keeping a paper ledger in the glovebox, but I’ve always beleived in tracking every hinge, screw, and spring by hand. It keeps me honest and, frankly, it slows my mind dowm enough to double-check the work. Garage door installation isn’t a flashy trade. It’s about patience, alignment, and respecting the home someone trusted you to improve.

When you call me for a new door I start with a proper walk-around. We look at the trim, the header, the slope of the driveway, even the bush that always seems to scrape the right panel. I share stories from decades on ladders, like the time a homeowner insisted on a bright teal door in 2002 and how we matched the hardware without clashing against old brick. These little chats matter because every door needs to fit the family, not just the frame. I never rush through that first visit, even if the wind is cutting through my jacket.

Once we settle on a style, I take exact measurements and order materials that can handle our wild Ontario seasons. Steel doors with insulated cores are still my go-to recommendation, but if you’ve dreamed about carriage-house panels or neat windows, I’ll make it happen. I explain R-values, track options, and opener compatability using plain words. Half the time we end up laughing at how complicated manufacturers make simple things sound. The goal is a door that runs smooth in February, July, and everything in between.

Installation day starts with canvas covers over your belongings. I remove the old door section by section, setting each piece down gently so nothing scuffs your garage walls. If I notice rot in the jambs or dents in the track, I fix them before the new hardware goes up. An extra forty-five minutes now saves you years of creaks later. My grandson sometimes helps on weekends, and I make sure he knows why we torque every bolt in a star pattern and check the balance twice.

With the new door assembled, we focus on balance and travel. I test the lift manually, then dial in the opener so it closes softly. A misaligned opener will grind gears faster than you’d expect, so I run it until I’m satisfied the chain or belt glides without snagging. I also review the auto-reverse system with you because safety sensors aren’t optional, they’re lifesavers. We place stickers with maintenance reminders, and I leave a handwritten card listing the first tune-up date.

Homeowners often tell me they appreciate the calm pace. After six decades on this earth, I’ve learnt there’s no prize for racing through good craftsmanship. I share easy maintenance tips: wipe down the weather seal, listen for squawks that hint at dry rollers, and call me if the door feels heavier than usual. I offer seasonal inspections because prevention costs less than emergency calls. You might hear me slip and say “aint” or “dont” during our chats, but you’ll also see the pride in my posture when that door closes flush and quiet.

One of my favourite parts is the reveal. We stand at the curb together and admire how the new door transforms the front of your home. Maybe the windows mirror your porch lights, or the walnut finish ties in with the fence. I snap a quick photo (with your permission) and jot a note about the colour so I can match touch-up paint if you ever need it. I’ll even adjust your exterior keypad or sync the opener to your vehicle while I’m there.

Long after I pack up, I remain a phone call away. If a grandchild bumps the bottom panel with a bicycle or you simply can’t remember how to lock out the opener during a vacation, I’ll swing by. Clients become friends because my approach is simple: do the job well, speak plainly, and treat people like neighbours. Your new garage door should feel sturdy, handsome, and reliable—and if something feels off, I’m the type to lose sleep until we make it right.

So whether you’re replacing a tired twenty-year-old door or finishing a brand-new build, lean on the experience of a fellow who’s hung more panels than he can count. Let’s pick materials that stand up to salt, ice, and summer storms. Let’s install insulation that keeps the workshop comfortable. And let’s enjoy that satisfying hush when your new door slides closed at the end of the day. Give me a ring, and we’ll get started the proper way.
